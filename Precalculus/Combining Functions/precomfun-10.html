Refer to the following tables. The first table provides information about function \(D(t)\), which gives the number of dogs (in millions) owned in the United States \(t\) years after 2000. The second table provides information about the number of cats \(C(t)\) as a function of the time \(t\) years after 2000.

Number of Dogs (in millions)
$$ \begin{array} {c|c} t & D(t) \\ \hline 1 & 3.4 \\ \hline 3 & 5.2 \\ \hline 5 & 5.9 \\ \hline 7 & 7.1 \\ \hline 9 & 8.5 \\ \hline \end{array} $$
Number of Cats (in millions)
$$ \begin{array} {c|c} t & C(t) \\ \hline 1 & 1.5 \\ \hline 3 & 2.3 \\ \hline 5 & 2.5 \\ \hline 7 & 2.8 \\ \hline 9 & 3.0 \\ \hline \end{array} $$

Which of the following is the best interpretation of \(D(C(9))\)?

Choose 1 Answer:

Approach

Although it may be tempting to evaluate the expression: $$D(C(9))$$ $$=D(3.0) $$ $$ = 8.5 $$

By including units, we see that this expression makes no sense.

\( C(9)\) refers to the number of cats in the year 2009. We can evaluate this to be 3 million cats.

It does not make sense to evaluate \(D(3)\), since the table does not give us the relationship between the number of cats and dogs. \(D(3)\) represents the number of dogs in year 2003.

Sidenote

Although certain combinations of functions do not work, we could easily evaluate some such as:

\(|D(9)-C(9)|\) refers to the positive difference between the number of cats and dogs in the year 2009

\(|D(9)+C(9)|\) refers to the the total number of cats and dogs in the year 2009
