Boron has two natural isotopes, boron-10 and boron-11. Which of the following is the best approximation of the percentage of all boron atoms on earth that are boron-11?

Choose 1 Answer:

Explanation

On the periodic table, the number below each symbol refers to the standard atomic weight, or just simply, atomic weight. To obtain this number, we calculate the weighted arithmetic mean of all naturally occuring isotopes of an element. For boron, with 2 isotopes, $$ \text{Atomic weight} = (\% \text{ abundance of isotope 1}) \times (\text{mass of isotope 1}) + (\% \text{ abundance of isotope 2}) \times (\text{mass of isotope 2}) $$ The mass of the two boron isotopes are 10 and 11. The % abundance is unknown for each, but we know they must add up to 100%. If we set the percent abundance of boron-11 to be \(p\), the percent abundance of boron-10 would be \(1-p\). $$ 10.81 = (p) \times (11) + (1-p) \times (10) $$ $$10.81=11p+10(1-p)$$ $$10.81=11p+10-10p$$ $$10.81=p+10$$ $$p=0.81$$ $$=\boxed{81\%} $$
